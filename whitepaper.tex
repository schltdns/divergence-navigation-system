\begin{abstract}
This paper introduces \textit{Triangulation nach Schult}, a structured, prompt-based multi-agent protocol designed to analyze epistemic divergence across Large Language Models (LLMs). Unlike consensus-oriented ensemble methods, the protocol treats disagreement, contradiction, and model refusal as informative signals. The method consists of six phases (P1--P6) that guide hypothesis formulation, parallel elicitation, divergence mapping, and human-centered synthesis. A preliminary case study (European gas market, April 2026) demonstrates the protocol's potential for uncertainty mapping and reflective decision-making. The approach is heuristic rather than formal, and aims to support human reasoning under uncertainty.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Large Language Models (LLMs) frequently converge due to shared training data, alignment constraints, and similar optimization objectives. This convergence can obscure systemic errors when multiple models confidently agree on incorrect assumptions.

The \textit{Triangulation nach Schult}\footnote{Note on Nomenclature: While the term 'Triangulation' is rooted in the sociological works of Denzin (1970), this protocol reframes divergence as a productive epistemic signal.} provides a structured process for:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Exposing blind spots
    \item Identifying bias patterns
    \item Stress-testing assumptions
    \item Supporting reflective synthesis
\end{itemize}
The method is not a truth-generating mechanism. It is a heuristic tool for analyzing uncertainty.
\section{Related Work}

The protocol intersects with several established research areas:

\subsection{Delphi Method (Dalkey \& Helmer, 1963)}
Iterative expert elicitation aiming for consensus. Our protocol differs by foregrounding divergence instead of reducing it.

\subsection{Ensemble Methods in Machine Learning}
Combine multiple models to improve predictive accuracy. They optimize for agreement, whereas our protocol optimizes for structured disagreement.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Debate (Du et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2024)}
LLMs critique each other in a council. Our protocol differs by using functional roles and explicit human synthesis.

\subsection{Red Teaming (Perez et al., 2022)}
Adversarial probing of a single model. Our protocol uses multiple heterogeneous models simultaneously.

\subsection{SCOUT (Uncertainty Triangulation)}
Uses model heterogeneity, stochastic inconsistency, and reasoning critique to predict LLM errors. It is fully automated; our protocol is human-centered.

\subsection{AI Council Framework}
Orchestrates models in a deliberative council to reach consensus. Our protocol treats divergence as a resource, not a problem.

\subsection{Semantic Triangulation}
Uses syntactic variations of the same problem to measure consistency. A statistical approach; our protocol is qualitative and role-based.

\subsection{DelphiAgent / LLM Council-Double Delphi}
Uses LLMs as a first expert round to create a problem map, followed by a human panel. Our protocol keeps the human operator involved throughout, and aims for non-falsification, not consensus.

Our protocol contributes a structured, role-based, prompt-level method for epistemic divergence analysis.
\subsection{Multi-Agent Orchestration Frameworks (AutoGen, CrewAI, OpenAI Swarm)}
Recent years have seen the emergence of powerful software libraries for orchestrating multiple LLM agents, such as Microsoft AutoGen, CrewAI, and OpenAI Swarm. These frameworks solve a technical problem: how to coordinate parallel or sequential agent interactions, define handoffs, and achieve task completion. They typically optimize for efficiency, autonomy, and consensus – often through mechanisms like majority voting, council debates, or hierarchical routing.

The \textit{Triangulation nach Schult} addresses a different, complementary problem. It is not a technical orchestration layer but an \textit{epistemic protocol}. Its goal is not to produce a single answer, but to systematically analyze divergence as a diagnostic signal. Where orchestration frameworks aim for \textit{consensus}, our protocol forces \textit{structured dissonance}. Where they automate error correction, our protocol treats model refusal and contradiction as valuable data points. Crucially, the protocol operates at a higher logical level: it can be implemented \textit{on top of} any orchestration framework that supports parallel model queries.

Thus, the frameworks provide the \textit{infrastructure} for multi-agent interaction, while the Triangulation nach Schult provides the \textit{epistemic logic} for interpreting disagreement and making human‑centered decisions under uncertainty.
\section{Method Overview}

\subsection{Core Principle}
Divergence is a signal, not noise.

The protocol uses multiple LLMs as perspective generators. Each model contributes a different epistemic function.

\subsection{Functional Roles (Illustrative Example)}
These roles are not fixed assignments but examples of functional differentiation:

\begin{tabular}{|l|l|}
\hline
\textbf{Role} & \textbf{Function} \\
\hline
Structural Model & Logical framing, decomposition \\
Boundary Model & Normative constraints, safety logic \\
Adversarial Model & Stress-testing, contradiction exposure \\
Didactic Model & Process reflection, explanation \\
Synthesis Model & Operational integration \\
Human Operator & Judgment, prioritization, meta-reflection \\
\hline
\end{tabular}

\section{The Six-Phase Protocol (P1--P6)}

\subsection{P1 — Hypothesis \& Falsification Setup}
Define hypotheses with explicit falsification thresholds. Example: H1 is rejected if variable X exceeds threshold T.

\subsection{P2 — Role-Based Model Configuration}
Assign models to functional roles. Roles are descriptive, not prescriptive.

\subsection{P3 — Parallel Elicitation}
All models receive the identical prompt. This ensures context constancy.

\subsection{P4 — Divergence Mapping}
Contradictions are categorized into:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Content divergence
    \item Methodological divergence
    \item Bias indicators
    \item Blind spots
    \item Refusals
\end{itemize}

\subsection{P5 — Weighted Synthesis}
The human operator integrates perspectives and justifies deviations from model outputs.

\subsection{P6 — Operator Self-Reflection}
Three guiding questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Which perspective dominated?
    \item Which perspective was ignored — and why?
    \item Which assumption remained unfalsified?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Falsification Rules (Operationalized)}

\subsection{Rule 1 — Iterative Divergence Rule}
If after \( N = 3 \) parallel elicitations the divergence pattern remains structurally identical (same contradiction class), the hypothesis is marked \textit{inconclusive} rather than falsified.

\subsection{Rule 2 — Refusal Rule}
If a model refuses with generic safety language, a substitute model is used. If two substitutes also refuse, the hypothesis is marked underspecified.

\subsection{Rule 3 — Human Justification Rule}
If the operator cannot justify ignoring a model’s argument, the synthesis is invalid.

\subsection{Rule 4 — External Review Rule}
A human reviewer evaluates the synthesis without knowing which model produced which output.

\section{Preliminary Case Study: European Gas Market (April 2026)}

A documented test was conducted on April 4, 2026, using three models (Grok, Claude, Copilot) with an identical prompt analyzing TTF gas prices and industrial resilience during the Hormuz crisis.

\subsection{Findings}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Grok and Claude partially confirmed the hypotheses; Copilot refused to answer as stated, requesting operational definitions.
    \item The refusal was treated as a meta-signal indicating underspecified hypotheses.
    \item The synthesis produced a coherent assessment: the market is driven by a real supply shock, with structural industrial adaptation.
    \item No falsification rule was triggered; the result is considered non-falsified but provisional.
\end{itemize}
This case demonstrates feasibility and sensitivity to divergence.

\section{Limitations}

The protocol:
\begin{itemize}
    \item does not quantify uncertainty
    \item does not guarantee correctness
    \item depends on human judgment
    \item requires careful prompt design
    \item is sensitive to model drift
    \item is not a formal algorithm
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

\textit{Triangulation nach Schult} is a structured, heuristic protocol for analyzing epistemic divergence across LLMs. It supports reflective decision-making by exposing contradictions rather than suppressing them. The method is preliminary but promising, and suitable for didactic, analytical, and exploratory contexts.
