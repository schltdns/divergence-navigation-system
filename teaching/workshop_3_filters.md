# Workshop: The Three Filters of the Labour Market 2030 – Using AI as a Resonating Body

**Target group:** Trainers, IHK examiners, vocational school teachers, HR professionals, mid‑level managers  
**Duration:** 90 minutes  
**Format:** Group work, plenary discussion, reflection  
**Prerequisites:** Basic understanding of AI (no technical depth needed)

---

## Learning Objectives

After the workshop, participants will be able to:
1. Explain the TNS master frame (tasks, three filters, power layer, master question)
2. Analyse a concrete job profile or business process through the three filters
3. Identify who gains, who loses, and who is excluded (power layer)
4. Derive concrete action steps for their own organisation or training practice

---

## Materials Needed

- Handout: Master Frame 3.3 (1 page – provided)
- Worksheet: Filter Check (1 page – provided)
- Flipchart/whiteboard or digital whiteboard (Miro, etc.)
- Optional: Access to an AI model (any) for live demonstration

## Agenda

| Time | Activity | Method |
|------|----------|--------|

## Handout: Master Frame 3.3 (1 page – give to participants)

### Core model

- **Unit of analysis:** **Tasks**, not whole jobs. A job can be 30% automated, 50% augmented, 20% new.
- **Bottleneck:** **Adaptive capacity** – technology scales exponentially, humans learn linearly, institutions react slowly.
- **Three filters:**  
  1. **Adoption** – Do firms redesign processes?  
  2. **Qualification** – Do people have the right skills and learning willingness?  
  3. **Institution** – Do regulations, curricula, and social partners enable or block change?
- **Power layer (4th filter):** Who controls AI? Who has access? Who profits? Who bears risks?
- **Master question:** *Does technology scale faster than social systems can adapt?*

### Key consensus findings (labour market 2030)

- Job change > job loss (20–40% of tasks transformed, net employment stable)
- Productivity potential 0.3–1.2% p.a. – **only if** adaptation happens
- Skill shift inevitable: routine analysis ↓, problem solving & AI literacy ↑
- Dual training is an advantage **if modernised quickly**
- Risks: inequality, regional divergence, age (50+), mental health

### Falsification example

> “If agent system adoption in companies stays below 10% by 2027, the exponential automation scenario is falsified.”

## Three Practical Cases (choose one per group)

### Case 1 – Administrative clerk (e.g., accounting, order processing)
- High exposure to AI automation (50–80% of tasks)
- Question: Can re‑skilling turn this job into a “process orchestrator” role?

### Case 2 – Skilled craft (e.g., electrician, mechatronics)
- Low to medium AI exposure (20–40% of tasks)
- Question: How can AI‑supported diagnostics and AR/VR training augment the job?

### Case 3 – Healthcare / nursing
- Low automation of core tasks (care, empathy), high automation of documentation
- Question: Who benefits from time freed up by AI documentation? Patients? Nurses? The hospital?

## Facilitation Notes

- **Encourage disagreement** – the goal is not consensus but making different assumptions visible.
- **Push for the power layer** – ask “Who is not in the room? Who loses? Who decides?”
- **Use the falsification mindset** – ask “What would have to happen for your conclusion to be wrong?”
- **Time management:** Keep group work strictly to 30 minutes. Use a timer.

---

## Follow‑up (optional)

- Participants can run the same filter check on their own department or training programme.
- Results can be shared as anonymous case studies in a follow‑up session.
